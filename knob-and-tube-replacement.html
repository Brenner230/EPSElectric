The Hidden Danger in Historic Homes

Knob and tube (K&T) wiring was the standard electrical installation method from the 1880s through the 1940s. While it was state-of-the-art for its time, it presents severe hazards for the modern homeowner in historic districts like Downtown Frederick, Capitol Hill, and Alexandria.

Why K&T Must Be Replaced

  • No Ground Wire: K&T systems only have a hot and neutral wire. Without a ground, electrical faults can shock you or destroy expensive appliances rather than safely tripping a breaker.
  • Degraded Insulation: The original rubber and cloth insulation around these wires becomes brittle over decades, flaking off and leaving bare, live wires exposed inside your walls and attic.
  • Overheating Risks: K&T wires were designed to be suspended in open air to dissipate heat. When modern homeowners blow insulation over K&T wiring in their attics, it traps the heat and creates a massive fire hazard.
  • Insurance & Mortgages: Most major home insurance companies will flat-out refuse to insure a property with active K&T wiring, making it nearly impossible to buy, sell, or refinance the home.

Knob & Tube FAQs

No, it is not illegal to have existing K&T wiring in your home, and there is no code requiring you to remove it just because it exists. However, it *is* a code violation to extend, modify, or add new outlets to a K&T circuit. Furthermore, the inability to get homeowner's insurance makes it a functional liability for most owners.

The easiest place to look is in an unfinished basement or attic. Look for ceramic white "knobs" nailed to the wooden joists, and ceramic "tubes" acting as sleeves where the wires pass through the wood. If you see two parallel wires running separately instead of bundled together in a plastic sheath, you likely have K&T.
