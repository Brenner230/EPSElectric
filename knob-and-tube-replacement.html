The Hidden Danger in Historic Homes

Knob and tube (K&T) wiring was the standard electrical installation method from the 1880s through the 1940s. While it was state-of-the-art for its time, it presents severe hazards for the modern homeowner in historic DMV districts like Downtown Frederick, Capitol Hill, and Alexandria. Hover or tap the cards below to see why it must be replaced.

No Ground Wire

K&T systems only have a hot and neutral wire. Without a ground, electrical faults can shock you or destroy expensive appliances rather than safely tripping a breaker.

Degraded Insulation

The original rubber and cloth insulation around these wires becomes brittle over decades, flaking off and leaving bare, live wires exposed inside your walls.

Heat Trapping

K&T was designed to be suspended in open air. If modern blown-in insulation covers it in an attic, it traps the heat and creates a massive fire hazard.

Uninsurable

Most major home insurance companies will flat-out refuse to bind a policy on a property with active K&T wiring, making it nearly impossible to buy or sell.

Historic Wiring FAQs

No, it is not illegal to have existing K&T wiring in your home, and there is no building code requiring you to remove it just because it exists. However, it is a code violation to extend, modify, or add new outlets to a K&T circuit. Furthermore, the inability to get homeowner's insurance makes it a functional liability for most owners.

The easiest place to look is in an unfinished basement or attic. Look for ceramic white "knobs" nailed to the wooden joists, and ceramic "tubes" acting as sleeves where the wires pass through the wood. If you see two parallel wires running separately instead of bundled together in a modern plastic sheath, you likely have K&T.

Installing GFCI outlets on an ungrounded K&T circuit is a temporary, NEC-approved code fix to prevent electrocution at the receptacle, but it does not solve the root problem. The wire itself is still old, brittle, and prone to overheating inside the walls, which is why insurance companies still demand its complete removal.
