The Flaw with Battery-Only Detectors

Standalone battery detectors have a critical flaw: if a fire starts in the basement at 2:00 AM, the basement alarm will sound, but you might not hear it in your second-story bedroom until it's too late. Maryland & D.C. Code Compliance

Maryland law specifically mandates that all homes be equipped with sealed, 10-year battery-backup smoke alarms. Furthermore, the National Electrical Code (NEC) requires hardwired, interconnected smoke alarms in every sleeping room, outside each separate sleeping area, and on every level of the home. Eminent Power Solutions ensures your home meets or exceeds these critical life-safety codes.

The EPS Master-Level Process

  • Strategic Placement: We map out your home's layout to ensure detectors are placed exactly where code dictates—avoiding "dead air" spaces in corners or too close to kitchen stoves where false alarms occur.

Safety Alarm FAQs

Even hardwired detectors have a battery backup. That "chirp" means the internal battery is dying and needs to be replaced. However, if the detector is over 10 years old, the chirp is a permanent "end-of-life" warning, meaning the entire unit must be replaced.

The NEC and local fire codes require a detector inside every bedroom, outside every sleeping area (like a central hallway), and at least one on every habitable level of the home, including the basement.

Ionization alarms are highly responsive to fast, flaming fires (like grease fires). Photoelectric alarms are better at detecting slow, smoldering fires (like a cigarette on a couch). For the absolute best protection, we install dual-sensor alarms that utilize both technologies in one unit.

Every smoke detector has a maximum lifespan of exactly 10 years from the date of manufacture. After a decade, the internal sensors degrade and can no longer be trusted to detect smoke. You must replace the entire physical unit.
