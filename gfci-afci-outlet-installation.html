Upgrading Your Home's Receptacles

Standard two-prong or basic three-prong outlets do not actively protect you from electrical faults. If your DMV home was built before the late 1990s, it likely lacks the specialized, active protection required by today's National Electrical Code (NEC). Eminent Power Solutions provides whole-home outlet retrofitting to keep your family safe. Hover or tap the cards below to understand the differences.

GFCI: Shock Defense

Ground Fault Circuit Interrupters (GFCI) cut power in 1/40th of a second if they sense electricity leaking to the ground, preventing severe electrocution.

AFCI: Fire Defense

Arc Fault Circuit Interrupters (AFCI) detect dangerous sparking behind your walls caused by damaged wires or loose connections before they can start a fire.

GFCI Locations

The NEC strictly requires GFCI protection in all "wet" areas: Kitchens, bathrooms, garages, unfinished basements, and exterior outdoor receptacles.

AFCI Locations

Modern code requires AFCI protection in almost all living spaces, including bedrooms, living rooms, dining rooms, hallways, and closets.

The Two-Prong Dilemma

If your older home still has two-prong outlets, simply swapping them for standard three-prong outlets is illegal and dangerous because there is no ground wire to protect your appliances. However, we can safely install GFCI receptacles at the beginning of the circuit to legally provide three-prong convenience while delivering massive shock protection, even without a true earth ground.

The EPS Master-Level Process

  • Breaker vs. Receptacle Analysis: Depending on your home's wiring, we determine if it's more cost-effective to install safety protection at the main electrical panel (via GFCI/AFCI breakers) or at the individual outlet level.
  • Circuit Tracing: In older homes, circuits often cross through multiple rooms. We trace the line to ensure a single GFCI outlet protects the entire downstream chain of standard outlets, saving you money.
  • Trip-Testing: We don't just wire it and leave. We use calibrated plug-in testers to physically force a ground fault to ensure the new device trips at the exact millisecond required by safety codes.
